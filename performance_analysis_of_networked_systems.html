CS 395T: Performance Analysis of Networked Systems (Spring 2024)


Instructor: Venkat Arun

About the course: Normally, we are constantly tweaking the design of computer systems in a never ending attempt to get them to perform well for the applications we care about. On the one hand, it creates guaranteed employment for systems people; something always needs fixing and innovation. However, it is also frustrating to revisit the same questions over and over again: Should we do this in software or hardware? Should this resource allocation algorithm be centralized or decentralized? What long list of "if conditions" do we need to make the Linux scheduler work? What network topology should we use? ...

Every so often, we find a unifying principle that lets us break out of this loop for some portion of a system. When we discover an algorithm or design that we can prove will always work, we can stop tweaking that component ad infinitum and focus our energy elsewhere. As systems get more complicated, and applications demand more reliable performance, it becomes more important that we stop making the same types of tweaks that we were making 20 years ago. This course will survey the success stories and try to learn unifying principles that will enable us to make such progress more quickly in the future.

Another objective of this course is to study what it takes to marry theory and practice. Theory can give us non-trivial insights that would be difficult to obtain in any other way. However, for it to be useful, we must understand the practical constraints so that we know what assumptions are reasonable and what results are useful.

The course is structured around lectures by the instructor and paper readings/presentations by the students with open discussion. Students will form a project group and conduct a research project combining theory and systems.

Syllabus: Applications of queuing and control theory, randomized load balancing and routing algorithms, notions of fairness, beyond worst-case analysis for online scheduling and caching algorithms, performance verification, use of constraint solvers, and practical implications of complexity theory.
